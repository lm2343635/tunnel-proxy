Tunnel Proxy — User Guide

A friendly guide to the Tunnel Proxy menu bar app for macOS.

What is Tunnel Proxy?

Tunnel Proxy is a small app that lives in your Mac's menu bar (the strip of icons at the top-right of the screen). It routes your internet traffic through your own SSH server over a secure, encrypted connection.

You might use it to:

  • Reach the internet through a server in another location.
  • Keep your traffic private on an untrusted network (like public Wi-Fi).
  • Make websites and apps see your server's IP address instead of your Mac's.

Everything it needs is built in — there is nothing else to install. You only need to know how to reach one SSH server (its address and how you sign in).

How it works

Your apps send their traffic to a local proxy running on your Mac. Tunnel Proxy carries that traffic to your SSH server through an encrypted tunnel, and the server passes it on to the internet. The websites you visit see the server's IP address, and everything between your Mac and the server is encrypted.

Installing the app

  1. Open the TunnelProxy.dmg file you received.
  2. Drag the Tunnel Proxy icon onto the Applications folder.
  3. Open Applications and double-click Tunnel Proxy.
    • The first time, macOS may ask "Are you sure you want to open it?" — click Open. (If macOS blocks it, go to System Settings → Privacy & Security, scroll down, and click Open Anyway.)
  4. A shield icon appears in the menu bar. There is no Dock icon and no window — the app lives entirely in the menu bar.

Tip: Don't see the shield? Your menu bar may be full. Hold ⌘ and drag some menu bar icons to make room, or check that the app is running.

First-time setup: add your server

Before you can connect, you need to tell the app about your SSH server.

  1. Click the shield icon → Settings… (or press ⌘,).
  2. Go to the Servers tab and click ＋ Add Server.

Fill in the details:

FieldWhat to enter
NameAny label you like, e.g. "Home server". Optional.
HostThe server's address, e.g. example.com or 203.0.113.10.
UsernameYour login name on the server, e.g. root.
PortThe SSH port. Usually 22 — leave it unless told otherwise.

Choose an Authentication method — how you prove who you are:

MethodWhen to use itWhat you enter
PasswordYou log in with a passwordYour SSH password
Private key fileYou have a key file (e.g. in ~/.ssh)Click Choose… to pick the key; add its passphrase if it has one
Default keys / agentYour Mac is already set up to connect with keysNothing — it uses your existing keys automatically

Click Save.

Your secrets are safe. Passwords and key passphrases are stored in the macOS Keychain, the same secure vault macOS uses for your other passwords. They are never written to a plain file.

You can add multiple servers and switch between them from the popover. In the Servers list, click the circle on the left to mark a server as the active one.

Connecting

  1. Click the shield icon.
  2. Make sure the right server is chosen in the Server row.
  3. Click Connect.

Within a few seconds the status turns green / Connected, and the Exit IP appears. That IP is your server's address — proof your traffic is going through the tunnel.

To stop, click the shield icon and press Disconnect.

The everyday switches

These live in the popover, below the Connect button.

Auto-reconnect (watchdog)

Keep this on (recommended). If the tunnel ever drops — say your Wi-Fi hiccups — the app quietly reconnects it for you in the background. You may briefly see the yellow "Reconnecting…" state while it does.

Show network speed

When on, live upload/download speeds appear next to the menu bar icon so you can see traffic flowing at a glance.

macOS SOCKS proxy

  • Off (default): Only apps you've pointed at the proxy use the tunnel.
  • On: The app configures your Mac's system-wide network settings so that all apps use the tunnel automatically.

Turn this on if you want everything on your Mac to go through the tunnel without configuring each app. When you disconnect or quit, remember to turn it back off if you want direct traffic again.

Launch at login

When on, Tunnel Proxy starts automatically each time you log in to your Mac, so the shield is always waiting in the menu bar.

There is also an Auto-connect on launch option in Settings → Advanced. Turn both on and your tunnel comes up by itself whenever you start your Mac.

Settings reference

Open with the shield icon → Settings… (⌘,). There are four tabs.

Servers

Add, edit, delete, and choose your active server. (See First-time setup.)

Tunnel

  • SOCKS Port / HTTP Proxy Port — the local port numbers the app listens on (defaults 1080 and 8118). Only change these if they clash with another program.
  • Network Service — which network connection (Wi-Fi, Ethernet…) the macOS SOCKS proxy switch applies to. Normally leave it on the default.
  • Watchdog Interval (s) — how often the auto-reconnect check runs.
  • Runtime — a green ✓ next to privoxy, ssh, and curl confirms everything the app needs is present.

Click Save after changing anything here.

Advanced

  • Launch at login and Auto-connect on launch (see above).
  • Files — shows where your config and log files live, with a Reveal in Finder button.

About

App name and a short description.

Viewing logs

If a connection fails or behaves oddly, the logs show exactly what happened.

  1. Click the shield icon → View Logs…
  2. The Logs window streams live:
    • Filter… — type to show only matching lines.
    • Auto-scroll — keeps the newest lines in view.
    • Clear — empties the display.

Lines are color-coded: green for success, orange for warnings, red for errors.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to install anything else (Homebrew, extra tools)?
No. Everything is built into the app. ssh and curl already come with macOS.

Is this a VPN?
It works similarly for your traffic, but it's an SSH tunnel to your own server, not a commercial VPN service. You provide the server.

Where is my password stored?
In the macOS Keychain — the secure system vault. Never in a plain text file.

Does it slow down my internet?
Your traffic takes an extra hop through your server, so there's a small overhead. The speed mostly depends on your server's connection.

How do I fully remove it?
Disconnect, turn off Launch at login and macOS SOCKS proxy, then quit and drag Tunnel Proxy from Applications to the Trash.

Need to check what's happening under the hood? The View Logs… window is always the best first stop.

Tunnel Proxy — User Guide

A friendly guide to the Tunnel Proxy menu bar app for macOS.

What is Tunnel Proxy?

Tunnel Proxy is an app for macOS that routes your internet traffic through your own SSH server over a secure, encrypted connection. It has a main window for everyday use and a compact menu bar shield icon (in the strip of icons at the top-right of the screen) for quick control.

You might use it to:

  • Reach the internet through a server in another location.
  • Keep your traffic private on an untrusted network (like public Wi-Fi).
  • Make websites and apps see your server's IP address instead of your Mac's.

Everything it needs is built in — there is nothing else to install. You only need to know how to reach one SSH server (its address and how you sign in).

How it works

Your apps send their traffic to a local proxy running on your Mac. Tunnel Proxy carries that traffic to your SSH server through an encrypted tunnel, and the server passes it on to the internet. The websites you visit see the server's IP address, and everything between your Mac and the server is encrypted.

Installing the app

  1. Open the TunnelProxy.dmg file you received.
  2. Drag the Tunnel Proxy icon onto the Applications folder.
  3. Open Applications and double-click Tunnel Proxy.
    • The first time, macOS may ask "Are you sure you want to open it?" — click Open. (If macOS blocks it, go to System Settings → Privacy & Security, scroll down, and click Open Anyway.)
  4. The main window opens, and a shield icon appears in the menu bar. You can control the app from either place.

Tip: Don't see the shield? Your menu bar may be full. Hold ⌘ and drag some menu bar icons to make room, or check that the app is running. You can reopen the main window any time from the menu bar popover's Open App button.

The main window

The main window is where you do most of your work. A sidebar on the left holds six tabs, and a small status card at the bottom of the sidebar shows your current connection and exit IP at a glance.

The Connection tab (dashboard)

Connection is the home page. It centers on a big power ring — click it to connect or disconnect. Around it you'll find live throughput and latency graphs, a card to quickly switch servers, and three one-tap option cards.

The three option cards below toggle the moment you click them — no Save needed:

  • Route Mac traffic — send all of your Mac's traffic through the tunnel (see below).
  • Auto-reconnect — quietly reconnect the tunnel if it ever drops.
  • Launch at login — start Tunnel Proxy automatically when you log in.

The menu bar popover

Click the shield icon in the menu bar to open a compact panel — handy when you don't want to open the whole window.

The popover packs the essentials into a small space:

  • Status card — the power ring (click to connect / disconnect), your exit IP, and current latency.
  • Speed & Server cards — live speed and the active server. Tap Switch ▾ to pick a different server.
  • Toggle cards — Route Mac traffic and Auto-reconnect, each a single tap.
  • Disconnect — stop the tunnel.
  • Open App / Quit — open the main window, or quit Tunnel Proxy.

The mini window

The mini window is a small, always-on-top floating panel that keeps your status and a Stop button in view while you work in other apps. Open it from the picture-in-picture icon in the popover's status card, or from the menu bar's View → Mini Window (⇧⌘M).

First-time setup: add your server

Before you can connect, you need to tell the app about your SSH server.

  1. Open the main window and select the Servers tab in the sidebar.
  2. Click ＋ Add Server.

Fill in the details:

FieldWhat to enter
NameAny label you like, e.g. "Home server". Optional.
HostThe server's address, e.g. example.com or 203.0.113.10.
UsernameYour login name on the server, e.g. root.
PortThe SSH port. Usually 22 — leave it unless told otherwise.

Choose an Authentication method — how you prove who you are:

MethodWhen to use itWhat you enter
PasswordYou log in with a passwordYour SSH password
Private key fileYou have a key file (e.g. in ~/.ssh)Click Choose… to pick the key; add its passphrase if it has one
Default keys / agentYour Mac is already set up to connect with keysNothing — it uses your existing keys automatically

Click Save.

Your secrets are safe. Passwords and key passphrases are stored in the macOS Keychain, the same secure vault macOS uses for your other passwords. They are never written to a plain file.

You can add multiple servers and switch between them from the Connection tab or the menu bar popover. In the Servers tab, click the circle on the left of a row to mark it as the active server.

Connecting

  1. Open the Connection tab (or click the shield icon for the popover).
  2. Make sure the right server is chosen in the Server card.
  3. Click the power ring.

Within a few seconds the status turns green / Connected, and the Exit IP appears. That IP is your server's address — proof your traffic is going through the tunnel.

To stop, click the power ring again (or press Disconnect in the popover).

The everyday options

These are the one-tap option cards on the Connection tab (a couple also appear as toggle cards in the popover). They apply the moment you click them.

Auto-reconnect

Keep this on (recommended). If the tunnel ever drops — say your Wi-Fi hiccups — the app quietly reconnects it for you in the background. You may briefly see the yellow "Reconnecting…" state while it does.

Route Mac traffic

  • Off (default): Only apps you've pointed at the proxy use the tunnel.
  • On: The app configures your Mac's system-wide network settings so that all apps use the tunnel automatically.

Turn this on if you want everything on your Mac to go through the tunnel without configuring each app. When you disconnect or quit, remember to turn it back off if you want direct traffic again.

Launch at login

When on, Tunnel Proxy starts automatically each time you log in to your Mac, so it's always ready in the menu bar.

There is also an Auto-connect on launch option in the Settings tab. Turn both on and your tunnel comes up by itself whenever you start your Mac.

Show network speed lives in the Settings tab, under Menu Bar. When on, live upload/download speeds appear next to the menu bar icon so you can see traffic flowing at a glance.

The Settings tab

The Settings tab gathers all the app's options, grouped into cards. There is no Save button — every change applies immediately. If you type an invalid value, an orange hint appears under that card.

  • Ports — SOCKS Port and HTTP Proxy Port, the local port numbers the app listens on (defaults 1080 and 8118). Only change these if they clash with another program.
  • Startup — Launch at login and Auto-connect on launch (see above).
  • macOS — the Network Service (Wi-Fi, Ethernet…) that Route Mac traffic applies to, the Auto-reconnect (watchdog) switch, and the Watchdog Interval (s) — how often the auto-reconnect check runs.
  • Menu Bar — Show menu bar icon and Show network speed.
  • Runtime — confirms the components the app needs (privoxy is bundled; ssh and curl come with macOS) are present.
  • Statistics — Record traffic statistics keeps a local record of how much data you send and receive over time (see Traffic statistics). It records byte volume only — never the sites you visit — and the data stays on your Mac. Clear statistics… permanently deletes that history.
  • Files — shows where your config and log files live, with a Reveal in Finder button.

The Tools tab

The Tools tab holds one-click helpers that point other programs at this app's HTTP proxy.

  • Route Claude Code through this proxy — turn this on to make Claude Code send its traffic through the tunnel. It adds the standard HTTP-proxy environment keys (http_proxy, https_proxy, and their uppercase forms, plus no_proxy for localhost) to ~/.claude/settings.json, pointing them at http://127.0.0.1:8118 (or whatever HTTP Proxy Port you set in the Settings tab). Turn it off to remove just those keys — everything else in your Claude Code settings is left untouched. Restart Claude Code for the change to take effect.
  • Settings file — shows the path to ~/.claude/settings.json, with a Reveal button.
  • Remove All Proxies — a rescue button that turns off every system proxy (HTTP, HTTPS, SOCKS, and automatic/PAC) for your current Network Service. Use it if proxy settings ever get left behind — for example after a crash — and your Mac can't reach the internet directly. It needs your admin password.

If you change the HTTP Proxy Port later, come back and toggle the Claude Code switch off and on again so the new port is written.

The Logs tab

If a connection fails or behaves oddly, the logs show exactly what happened.

  1. Open the Logs tab in the sidebar.
  2. The log streams live:
    • Filter… — type to show only matching lines.
    • Auto-scroll — keeps the newest lines in view.
    • Clear — empties the display.

Lines are color-coded: green for success, orange for warnings, red for errors.

The Statistics tab

Tunnel Proxy can keep a running tally of how much data flows through the tunnel, so you can see your usage over time.

Turn it on with Record traffic statistics in the Settings tab. It records the amount of data (bytes up and down) only — it does not log which websites you visit — and everything is stored locally on your Mac.

Open the Statistics tab to see:

  • Range — switch between Today, Week, Month, and All.
  • Server filter — narrow the session list to a single server, or show all.
  • Totals — headline Downloaded, Uploaded, and Total figures for the chosen range.
  • Usage over time — a bar chart of download (blue) and upload (green) volume, by hour for Today and by day for longer ranges.
  • Sessions — each connection, with its time span, which server it used, and how much it moved. A green dot marks a session that's still open.
  • Export… — save the chart data as a .csv file for use in a spreadsheet.

Recording is off until you turn it on, so early sessions won't appear. The status bar at the bottom tells you whether recording is on and since when. Use Clear statistics… in the Settings tab to erase the history.

Troubleshooting

ProblemTry this
Power ring won't connectYou haven't added a server yet, or the settings are incomplete. Open the Servers tab and check the details.
Stuck on "Connecting…" then ErrorCheck the Host, Port, and Username. Make sure you can reach the server (try connecting from Terminal). Open the Logs tab for the exact error.
Wrong password / keyRe-open the server in the Servers tab, click the pencil, and re-enter your password or re-choose your key file.
Websites still show my real IPMake sure the status is green / Connected. If an app ignores the proxy, turn on Route Mac traffic so the whole system uses the tunnel.

"Bundled proxy missing"The app files are incomplete. Re-download and reinstall from the DMG.
No shield icon after launchYour menu bar may be full — ⌘-drag other icons aside. Confirm the app is running.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to install anything else (Homebrew, extra tools)?
No. Everything is built into the app. ssh and curl already come with macOS.

Is this a VPN?
It works similarly for your traffic, but it's an SSH tunnel to your own server, not a commercial VPN service. You provide the server.

Where is my password stored?
In the macOS Keychain — the secure system vault. Never in a plain text file.

Does it slow down my internet?
Your traffic takes an extra hop through your server, so there's a small overhead. The speed mostly depends on your server's connection.

How do I fully remove it?
Disconnect, turn off Launch at login and Route Mac traffic, then quit and drag Tunnel Proxy from Applications to the Trash.

Need to check what's happening under the hood? The Logs tab is always the best first stop.
